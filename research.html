The Effects of Minimum Wage Increases on Poverty and Food Hardship

(with Lukas Lehner, Zachary Parolin, Rafael Pintro Schmitt)

IZA Discussion Paper 18142

Abstract. We study how minimum wage (MW) increases affect poverty and food hardship in the United States from 1981–2019. Applying stacked difference-in-difference models and the Supplemental Poverty Measure (SPM), we find that a $1 MW increase reduces poverty by 0.3 to 0.7 percentage points among all working-age adults, and by 1.2 to 1.7 percentage points among individuals most likely to work in MW jobs. We also find that a $1 MW increase reduces food insufficiency by 1.5 percentage points among likely-MW workers. Effects on poverty are partially offset by higher living costs in MW-increasing states. Our findings are robust across methodological choices that have divided the recent literature. Overall, MW increases meaningfully reduce poverty and food hardship for the workers most directly affected and deliver modest improvements for the broader working-age population.

(Mis)perceptions of Inequality and Their Determinants

(with Franetovic, Löffler, Memmen, Moya, Schmitt)

Abstract. We study how people perceive and evaluate wealth inequality in Germany—one of Western Europe’s most unequal wealth distributions. Using an online survey with a factorial vignette experiment, respondents (Prolific, quota-sampled) (i) estimate euro amounts at key percentiles of the wealth distribution and (ii) place hypothetical individuals into percentiles and judge whether their wealth is unfairly low, fair, or unfairly high. Vignettes vary effort (hours worked), inheritance, and gender while holding wealth amounts fixed. We test five hypotheses: systematic underestimation of inequality (especially at the top); meritocratic attribution raises perceived deservingness; gender bias favors men; non-meritocratic wealth (inheritance) is penalized in fairness judgments; and self-location biases perceptions and evaluations. Role of education in the era of automation: A task-based approach

Hannah Massenbauer

Abstract. Recent technological advancements have evoked discussion about substitution and complementation of workers’ tasks. The skill-biased technological change hypothesis focused on the role of higher education. A newer approach—the routine-biased technological change hypothesis—focuses on a job’s task composition, in particular the routine task intensity (RTI). This paper examines the link between higher education and RTI and how these two factors are intertwined. Using data from the European Social Survey covering 18 European countries from 2012 to 2018, I find that higher education significantly reduces RTI by 20%. This implies that higher educated people face less risk of workplace automation and could benefit from complementary usage. However, the extent to which higher education reduces routine-intense tasks varies between European countries and demographic subgroups such as gender and income deciles. A dynamic analysis reveals a divergence of RTI across Europe, with implications for inequality and policy.
